The Monty Hall Problem

The Monty Hall Problem is an excellent example of the way that probability can often be unintuitive. In this problem, you have three doors, behind one of which there is a car. This problem often stumps new students who neglect to understand how having an option to stay or switch will effect your final odds. Use the simulator below to understand how this problem operates. You can also use the table below to run any number under 1500 rounds automatically to see the averages of your outcomes.

Welcome to the Monty Hall Problem!

Behind two of these doors there are goats. Behind one of the doors, there is a car. During this demonstration, you will be able to first select one of the three doors. Once you have chosen, one of the doors that you did not choose will be lifted. You must choose to either stay with your original choice or switch to the door that you did not pick.

Which strategy is the best?

In the Monty Hall problem, switching will give you the best opportunity to win a car. If you switch, you would win approximately \(\frac{2}{3}\) of the time, while if you stay, you would only win \(\frac{2}{3}\) of the time.

Imagine that you choose the car door correctly, which happens \(\frac{1}{3}\) of the time. You will then be shown one of the two goats at random. If you stay, you win.

Now, imagine that the door you choose originally is a goat, which happens \(\frac{2}{3}\) of the time. Then you will be shown the other goat. In this case, you must switch to win.
