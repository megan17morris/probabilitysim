Letter Mix Up

If you are given a word and asked to find how many different ways can you reorder the letters, how would you solve the problem? Would you try to find every combination of letters? Well if there's at least 5 letters in your word, you may have 120 combinations to find and if you had more letters to the word, this number is only going to grow and grow. And I personally would rather not write out every combination. This lesson will walk you through how to solve this kind of problem.

Example

Words with No Repeating Letters

Let's say that we want to find the number of ways we can permute the letters of the word DESK.

We can imagine setting down the letters in a line. When we are ready to choose our first letter, we have 4 choices. When we choose our third letter, we have already used one of the letters so we have 3 options left. When we choose our third letter, there will be 2 letters left to choose. When we set down the last letter, all others will have been used so we will have only one option left. This implies that we will have \(4 *3 * 2 * 1 = 24 \) possible ways of permuting the letters of "DESK". We model this example in the following diagram.

Example

Words With Repeating Letters

Now we will consider the situation when we want to rearrange letters of a word, but this time the word has the same letter or letters multiple times contained within it. Let's use the word MOOD as an example.

Looking at the word MOOD, we see that the word has 1 M, 2 O's and 1 D. If we try to swap the two O's in the center of the word, we would just get the same word. To control for these repeating letters, we will follow the same process as the last example, but this time, we will divide our entire answer by 2 as there are 2 O's in MOOD.

The General Example

We have seen a few examples now so let's investigate the general formula.

As we did in the first example, we will always take the factorial of the number of letters in the word. We can then sort the word into groups of letters. For example, the word Bumblebee contains 2 B's, 1 U, 1 M, 1 L and 3 E's. We will then divide the original factorial by the product of the factorials of the number of letters in each group. If this is hard to understand, please examine the formula below.

In the formula below, N represents the total number of letters in the word. nA represents the numbers of A's in the word, nB represents the number of B's in the word and so on. Enter in your own word to see how many combinations of letters there are.

The simulator will not accept any phrases with spaces, and will reject non-alphabetical letters. There is also a limit of 20 letters per word but you will only see a maximum of 100 different combinations.
