The Birthday Problem

Let's assume that the chance of having a birthday on a certain day is equal for every day of the year. How many people need to enter a room before the probability that two individuals share a birthday is less than 50%? Read the explanations below and the interactive slider provided to find out.

The Case for 2 People

If there is only 1 person in the room, the birthday problem is trivial. There is a 0% chance of two people sharing a birthday since there is only 1 person.

If there are only 2 people in the room, the problem is still simple. The probability that the 2 people share a birthday is 1⁄365 as the second person in the room has a 1 out of 365 chance of being born on the same day as the first person.

2 People

P(The 2 people share a birthday) = $$ 1 * \frac{1}{365} = \frac{1}{365} $$

A Case of 3 People

Imagine Ashley, Bob, and Charlie enter the room in that order.

How do we figure out the probability that at least two of them share a birthday?

We can first add the probability that each pair of people share a birthday.

But wait! if we do that, then we also have to account for the fact that adding in this way means that we added the probability of all 3 sharing a birthday three times, so we must subtract out that probability.

Rather complicated right? Let's move to the generalized solution to see how we can make this problem easier.

3 People

P(At least 2 out of 3 share a birthday) =

P(A & B) + P(B & C) + P(A & C) - 2 × P(A & B & C) =

$$\frac{1}{365} + \frac{1}{365} + \frac{1}{365} - 2((\frac{1}{365})^{2})$$

The Generalized Case

As you can see with the case of three people, this problem becomes rather difficult quickly if we don't find a simpler way to approach it.

Instead of trying to find the probabilities of any given two people sharing a birthday, we can use a trick of probability to simplify. Instead, consider the probability that no two people in the room share a birthday.

What does this mean? If no two people share a birthday, that means that everyone in the room has a distinct birthday. While this is the opposite of what we are trying to find, we can use the fact that 1 minus the probability of an event not happening is equal to the event happening. Look below to see how this applies to the case of N people.

N people

1 - P(No one shares a birthday) = $$ 1 - (1 * \frac{364}{365} * ... \frac{365 - (n-1)}{365} ) $$
